# Honeycomb 3D Instance

**The {4,3,5} BFS star patch — 32 cubic cells, the first 3D holographic bridge instance.**

**Modules:** `Common/Honeycomb3DSpec.agda` (AUTO-GEN), `Boundary/Honeycomb3DCut.agda` (AUTO-GEN), `Bulk/Honeycomb3DChain.agda` (AUTO-GEN), `Bulk/Honeycomb3DCurvature.agda` (AUTO-GEN), `Bridge/Honeycomb3DObs.agda` (AUTO-GEN), `Bridge/Honeycomb3DEquiv.agda`

**Role:** The {4,3,5} BFS star patch is the first 3D instance in the repository, demonstrating that the holographic bridge architecture is dimension-agnostic. It transitions the formalization from 1D boundary / 2D bulk (the tree and pentagonal patches) to 2D boundary / 3D bulk — the dimensional regime where holographic duality was originally formulated by Maldacena (1997). The patch validates the generic bridge pattern on a genuinely 3D cell complex where min-cuts correspond to minimal separating *surfaces* (sets of 2D faces), not minimal separating *chains* (sets of 1D edges as in 2D).

## 2. Topology

The {4,3,5} honeycomb is the natural 3D successor to the {5,4} pentagonal tiling. Regular cubes tile hyperbolic 3-space with **5 cubes meeting at every edge** — the Schläfli symbol {4,3,5} encodes cubes ({4,3}) with edge valence 5 ({⋯,5}). The dihedral angle of a cube is π/2 = 90°, and 5 × 90° = 450° > 360°, giving a negative angular deficit of −90° = −π/2 per fully-surrounded edge. This is the source of 3D hyperbolic curvature.

The BFS star patch is grown by breadth-first search from the central cube in the cell-adjacency graph of the {4,3,5} honeycomb, using Coxeter group reflections in the simple root basis of the [4,3,5] Coxeter group. The Coxeter group has 4 generators with Gram matrix signature (3,1), confirming a compact hyperbolic group. The cell stabilizer is the octahedral group [4,3] of order 48, and each cube has 6 face-crossings (one per face).

**Coxeter geometry (from `05_honeycomb_3d_prototype.py`):**

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Coxeter group | [4,3,5] with 4 generators |
| Gram matrix signature | (3,1) — compact hyperbolic |
| Cell stabilizer | [4,3] — order 48 (octahedral) |
| Face crossings per cell | 6 |
| Edge valence (full honeycomb) | 5 at all 12 edges of the central cube |
| Dihedral angle | π/2 = 90° |

**Patch structure:**

| Count | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Cells (cubes) | 32 |
| Internal shared faces | 31 |
| Boundary faces | 130 |
| Faces per cell | 6 |

The 32-cell BFS star patch saturates all 12 edges of the central cube to the full {4,3,5} valence of 5, meaning every edge of the central cube is surrounded by exactly 5 cubes from the patch. This confirms genuine 3D hyperbolic geometry within the patch — the first such confirmation in the repository.

## 3. Boundary Regions

The boundary regions are cell-aligned connected subsets of boundary cells (cells with at least one exposed face). For the BFS star patch, the generated Agda module `Common/Honeycomb3DSpec.agda` enumerates 26 singleton-cell boundary regions — each consisting of a single boundary cell with 5 exposed faces (one face shared with the interior, 5 facing outward).

**Region type:** `H3Region` with 26 constructors (`h3r0` through `h3r25`).

**Min-cut value distribution:**

| Min-cut value | Count |
|---------------|-------|
| S = 1 | 26 regions (all singletons) |

All 26 boundary regions have min-cut = 1 because each is a single cell connected to the interior through exactly one shared face. Severing that single face disconnects the region from its complement. This is the BFS-shell property: every boundary cell connects to the interior through ~1 face, so min-cut ≈ 1 for all singletons.

**Comparison with Dense patches:** The Dense-50 patch (same tiling, greedy max-connectivity growth) produces 139 regions with min-cut values ranging from 1 to 7. The BFS star's uniform min-cut of 1 is a consequence of its concentric-shell topology, not a limitation of the 3D architecture. The generic bridge theorem handles both topologies identically — it depends only on the `PatchData` interface, not on the min-cut value distribution.

This is the 3D discrete Ryu–Takayanagi correspondence: boundary entanglement entropy equals bulk minimal surface area.

## 5. 3D Edge Curvature

In 3 dimensions, the combinatorial curvature lives on **edges** (not vertices as in 2D). At an interior edge e of a polyhedral 3-complex with valence v (number of cells meeting at the edge):

> κ(e) = 2π − v · (dihedral angle of cell)

For the {4,3,5} honeycomb (5 cubes at every edge, dihedral angle π/2):

> κ(e) = 2π − 5 · π/2 = −π/2

This negative edge deficit confirms the hyperbolic character of the honeycomb: too many cubes meet at each edge, creating an angular excess.

The edge curvature is encoded in twentieths (via `ℚ₁₀ = ℤ`) in `Bulk/Honeycomb3DCurvature.agda`:

> κ₂₀(e) = 20 − 5 · v

| Edge class | Valence in patch | Count | κ₂₀ | Interpretation |
|------------|-----------------|-------|------|----------------|
| `ev8` | 8 | 4 | −20 | Hyperbolic (8 cubes from dihedral orbit in patch) |
| `ev10` | 10 | 8 | −30 | Hyperbolic (10 cubes from dihedral orbit in patch) |

**Note on in-patch valence:** The edge valences 8 and 10 are the counts of dihedral-orbit cells present in the patch, not the honeycomb valence (which is 5). The dihedral group at each edge has order 10, producing an orbit of 5 distinct cell centers in the full honeycomb. The in-patch valence counts how many of those orbit positions are actually occupied by cells in the 32-cell BFS patch, which can exceed 5 because the BFS growth produces cells at multiple dihedral-orbit positions.

**Total edge curvature:**

```
totalH3Curvature = 4 ·₁₀ (negsuc 19) +₁₀ 8 ·₁₀ (negsuc 29)
                 = negsuc 319    (= −320 in twentieths)
```

Verified by `refl` in `Bulk/Honeycomb3DCurvature.agda`:

```agda
totalH3Curvature-check : totalH3Curvature ≡ negsuc 319
totalH3Curvature-check = refl
```

## 6. Bridge Construction

### 6.1 Pointwise Agreement

**Pointwise agreement** (`Bridge/Honeycomb3DObs.agda`):

```agda
h3-pointwise :
  (r : H3Region) →
  S-cut h3BdyView r ≡ L-min h3BulkView r
h3-pointwise h3r0  = refl
h3-pointwise h3r1  = refl
  ...  -- 26 cases, all refl
h3-pointwise h3r25 = refl
```

**Function path** (`Bridge/Honeycomb3DObs.agda`):

```agda
h3-obs-path : S-cut h3BdyView ≡ L-min h3BulkView
h3-obs-path = funExt h3-pointwise
```

**Package path** (`Bridge/Honeycomb3DObs.agda`):

```agda
h3-package-path : H3Obs∂ ≡ H3ObsBulk
h3-package-path i .ObsPackage.obs = h3-obs-path i
```

The honeycomb patch is retroactively validated as an instantiation of `GenericEnriched` in `Bridge/GenericValidation.agda`:

## 10. What the Honeycomb 3D Patch Does NOT Exercise

- **Orbit reduction.** The honeycomb BFS star has only 26 regions with a single min-cut value (all S = 1) — orbit reduction is not needed. Orbit reduction is first used by Dense-100 ([`instances/dense-100.md`](dense-100.md)) with 717 → 8 orbits.

- **Non-trivial min-cut range.** All 26 regions have S = 1. Non-trivial min-cut ranges (2–7, 1–8, 1–9) require the Dense growth strategy: Dense-50 ([`instances/dense-50.md`](dense-50.md)) achieves min-cut up to 7, and Dense-100 up to 8.

- **Area law / Bekenstein–Hawking bound.** The discrete area law S ≤ area and the half-bound 2·S ≤ area are first verified on Dense-100 ([`instances/dense-100.md`](dense-100.md)).

- **`abstract` barrier.** The 26-case pointwise proof is small enough to normalize quickly without sealing. The `abstract` pattern is first used by the 11-tile filled patch ([`instances/filled-patch.md`](filled-patch.md)) with 360 subadditivity cases.

- **Parameterized step invariance / dynamics.** The dynamics layer operates on the 6-tile star with parameterized weights. See [`instances/star-patch.md`](star-patch.md).

- **Gauge connections / quantum superposition.** The gauge and quantum layers are exercised on the 6-tile star. See [`instances/star-patch.md`](star-patch.md).

- **Positive curvature.** The honeycomb has negative edge curvature. Positive curvature requires the {5,3} de Sitter patch ([`instances/desitter-patch.md`](desitter-patch.md)).

- **2D polygon complex / vertex curvature.** The honeycomb is 3D with edge curvature; the 2D vertex curvature and Gauss–Bonnet theorem are exercised by the filled patch ([`instances/filled-patch.md`](filled-patch.md)).

## 12. Historical Significance

The honeycomb 3D patch is the repository's **first 3D bridge calibration object** — the dimensional successor to the 2D star and filled patches. It was developed as Direction D of the frontier extensions (§6 of `historical/development-docs/10-frontier.md`), which identified the {4,3,5} honeycomb as the natural 3D generalization of the {5,4} pentagonal tiling.

The key architectural insight: **the bridge is dimension-agnostic**. The `GenericEnriched` module from `Bridge/GenericBridge.agda` depends on exactly four inputs — a region type, two observable functions, and a path between them. It never inspects whether the patch is 2D or 3D, whether bonds represent shared edges (2D) or shared faces (3D), or whether curvature lives on vertices or edges. The honeycomb patch validates this claim concretely: the same 30-line generic proof that works for the 10-region star also works for the 26-region 3D honeycomb.

The feasibility prototype (script 05) established that the {4,3,5} honeycomb satisfies all gate criteria for formalization:

- **Boundary faces 100–500:** 130 (✓)
- **At least one fully-surrounded edge:** All 12 central-cube edges at full valence 5 (✓)
- **Orbit count ≤ 200:** 1 (trivially, since all min-cuts are equal) (✓)

The multi-strategy comparison (script 07) revealed that the BFS-shell topology produces uniform min-cut = 1 (every boundary cell connects through a single face), while the Dense growth strategy produces genuinely non-trivial min-cut values — motivating the Dense-50 and Dense-100 instances that followed.

## 14. The Dimensional Transition

The honeycomb patch demonstrates the following dimensional transition in the holographic correspondence:

| Property | Tree (1D) | Star/Filled (2D) | Honeycomb (3D) |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| Cell type | Edges | Pentagons | Cubes |
| Faces per cell | — | 5 | 6 |
| Min-cut separates | Vertices (0D) | Edges (1D) | Faces (2D) |
| Curvature lives on | — | Vertices | Edges |
| Curvature formula | — | κ(v) = 1 − deg/2 + Σ(1/sides) | κ(e) = 2π − v·(π/2) |
| Gauss–Bonnet | — | Σ κ(v) = χ(K) | (not formalized for 3D χ) |
| Bridge proof | `refl` on ℕ | `refl` on ℕ | `refl` on ℕ |
| Bridge architecture | `GenericEnriched` | `GenericEnriched` | `GenericEnriched` |

The last two rows are the key insight: **the bridge proof is identical at every dimension**. The `refl`-based pointwise agreement, the `funExt` assembly, and the `GenericEnriched` equivalence construction operate on abstract `PatchData` — they never know or care whether they are processing 1D edges, 2D pentagons, or 3D cubes. This is the structural content of the claim that the holographic correspondence is a property of information flow (the flow graph), not of geometry (the embedding dimension).
